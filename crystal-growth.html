A firsthand investigation into how potassium aluminium sulfate nucleates and grows from a supersaturated solution — observing growth rate, shape, and clarity over multiple sessions.

Introduction

Why Study Crystal Growth?

In this study I grew a crystal using a solution of potassium aluminium sulfate (KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O), which is commonly used to grow crystals, and water (H₂O). I chose this experiment to study how crystals form. During the study I observed and documented the growth rate, shape, and clarity of the crystal and of any residual crystals.

Crystals form as molecules arrange themselves into a repeating, ordered pattern — a rare glimpse of chemistry made visible.

Elements and chemicals used during this study include water (H₂O) and potassium aluminium sulfate (KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O). The concentration of the solution was 1 g of potassium aluminium sulfate per 2 g of water. To fully dissolve the potassium alum, the water was heated to 60 °C. The crystal was then hung in the solution contained within a beaker to result in uniform growth.

To create the seed crystal, I created a small amount of solution and poured it into a large beaker, filling it to about the height of a petri dish. To hang the crystal it is recommended to use nylon fishing wire; however, the lab did not have that on hand, so I had to use copper wire. I checked the crystal on Tuesdays and Thursdays.

Lab Note

Nylon is preferred for suspension because it is inert and does not react with the solution. Copper wire was used as a substitute; any effect on crystal purity or geometry was not formally measured in this study.

Discussion

Why Did the Crystal Form This Way?

The crystal formed this way because, as the supersaturated solution cooled, the alum molecules combined into a repeating pattern. Some factors that likely impacted the growth were dust build-up in the solution — which can halt or disrupt growth — alongside the addition of dye to the solution.

A limitation I faced was having only one small beaker in which to grow the crystals. This constrained the overall size of the final specimen and may have contributed to the irregular geometry observed.

If I were to do this again, I would place a seed crystal on the bottom of the beaker and then hang another above it to create a taller, more uniform crystal.
